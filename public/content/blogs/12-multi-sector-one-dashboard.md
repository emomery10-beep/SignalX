# Running a Restaurant AND a Repair Shop? One Dashboard Now Covers Both

**The POS industry has always assumed your business fits neatly into one category. Most real businesses don't.**

---

## The Mixed Business Problem

Walk into the right kind of independent business and you'll find operations that don't fit the industry's taxonomy.

A garage that fixes cars also runs a parts retail counter where customers can buy filters, oils, and accessories. A café that serves coffee and food also sells branded mugs, bags of beans, and merchandise. A nail salon that does treatments also retails polishes, tools, and skincare products. A phone repair shop that fixes devices also sells accessories, cases, and refurbished handsets.

These aren't unusual businesses. They're extremely common — especially among the kind of entrepreneurial owner-operators who are always looking for another revenue stream to run alongside their core operation.

But for decades, POS software has forced them to make an uncomfortable choice: pick one sector and cram everything else into it (creating all kinds of reporting chaos), or buy and run two separate systems (creating all kinds of cost and management complexity).

---

## Why Running Two Systems Is Quietly Awful

The two-systems approach sounds reasonable until you live with it.

You have two separate logins, two separate dashboards, two separate staff setups. When you want to know how the business performed last week across both operations, you pull numbers from two places and add them up manually. When staff work across both areas (as they often do in a small business), you're either giving them access to both systems or asking them to log in and out.

Revenue reporting gets fragmented. If your repair shop and your retail counter are in separate systems, your total business picture lives in a spreadsheet you build yourself once a month, probably on a Sunday evening when you'd rather be doing something else.

Meanwhile, you're paying for two subscriptions. At Square or Lightspeed pricing, that's £100–300/month in software fees alone, before transaction fees.

## The Staff Assignment Model

Assigning a staff member to a sector is done by the admin. Once assigned, the staff member's login at pos.askbiz.co automatically presents the correct sector interface — they don't choose it, they don't need to understand the backend, they just see their work environment.

There's a deliberate limit on how many times a sector assignment can be changed: two changes maximum. This prevents a pattern of constant reassignment that typically signals either poor planning or account-sharing (one login being used by multiple people in different roles). If you genuinely need to reassign a staff member permanently beyond that limit, you provision a new seat — which also means you maintain a clean record of who was doing what, and when.

For a business owner managing a mixed operation with staff who have different roles in different parts of the business, this creates a clear organisational structure that the software enforces automatically.

## A Real Scenario: The Garage and the Retail Counter

Imagine a garage in a mid-sized UK town. The core business is vehicle repair — MOTs, servicing, and bodywork. They employ four mechanics and one front-of-house staff member.

Three years ago, the owner converted a section of the forecourt into a small retail operation selling car care products, accessories, and novelty items. It's run by one part-time member of staff.

Currently they run two systems: their garage management software handles repairs, and a basic retail POS handles the retail counter. The owner reconciles the two every week manually. Staff sometimes cover both areas during quiet periods, which means they're either locked out of the system they need or being given access to systems they don't need.

On AskBiz: the garage runs as the Repair sector. The retail counter runs as the Retail sector. The owner sees both from one dashboard, filtered by sector when needed, or combined for total business view. The mechanic who occasionally covers the retail counter gets a second seat assignment in Retail — or the front-of-house staff covers it, with their own seat. The cross-sector revenue picture is available in real time, not assembled manually once a week.

## The Reporting Payoff

The practical value of multi-sector intelligence from one system becomes clear when you look at the questions it lets you answer that you couldn't before.

Which sector drove more revenue last month — the café or the merchandise? In a two-system world, you'd calculate this manually. In AskBiz, you filter by sector in the Overview tab and the answer is there.

Which day of the week is the repair business busiest? Is it the same day as the retail counter's peak, creating staffing pressure — or different, creating an opportunity to share staff efficiently? This kind of cross-sector operational insight only exists if both sectors are in the same reporting environment.

Is the salon part of the business dragging down overall margin, or is it your best performer? You can't know that if salon revenue lives in one system and retail revenue lives in another.
