# The Real Cost of Your POS System: What American Small Business Owners Aren't Told

**The sticker price on your POS system is the least of what you'll pay — and by the time most business owners figure that out, they're locked in.**

## Let's Do the Actual Maths

Take a small independent restaurant. Eight staff. One location. Doing around $800,000 a year in revenue — about $67,000 a month. Not a huge operation. The kind of place that's the backbone of American dining.

On Toast, the restaurant POS market leader, here's what that looks like:

- Toast POS software: $165/month (Point of Sale plan)
- Hardware starter kit: $627 upfront (one terminal, one card reader)
- Toast Payments processing: 2.49% + 15¢ per transaction

On $67,000 of monthly card revenue, the processing fee alone is around $1,685. Add the monthly subscription, and you're paying roughly $1,850 per month — $22,200 a year — to run a single-location restaurant POS.

That is not an unusual number. Toast's own pricing page lays this out, though you have to read carefully and do the multiplication yourself. They don't present the annualised total.

---

## Square Isn't Simpler

Square markets itself as the affordable option, and for a very small business with very low transaction volume, it might be. But scale it to anything resembling a real operation and the maths changes.

Square for Restaurants charges $60/month per location. Card processing is 2.6% + 10¢ per card-present transaction. On $67,000 monthly revenue, processing fees run approximately $1,752. Monthly total: around $1,812 — nearly identical to Toast.

Square also charges for additional features that should arguably be standard: online ordering, loyalty programmes, team management beyond the basics. These add-ons typically run $25–50/month each.

Clover, which is pushed heavily by banks and payment processors, has a similar fee structure with the added complication that its hardware is often leased rather than purchased — meaning you're paying a monthly hardware fee on top of everything else.

---

## The Hidden Cost Structure

What all of these platforms share is a dual revenue model: they make money on the monthly subscription, and they make money on every transaction you process. The transaction fee is the more valuable revenue stream, which is why these platforms compete aggressively on subscription pricing while quietly taking 2–3% of every dollar you turn.

For a business with thin margins — and restaurants, cafés, and independent retail all run on margins that would alarm most people — that 2–3% is not a rounding error. It is a meaningful fraction of net profit.

Consider: a restaurant with 10% net profit margin on $800,000 revenue is making $80,000/year. Paying $22,000/year in POS fees means the POS system is consuming more than a quarter of net profit. Framed that way, it's extraordinary.

## The Multi-Business Owner

Here's a scenario the major platforms have almost entirely failed to accommodate: the American small business owner who runs more than one type of business.

This is more common than the POS industry acknowledges. A barbershop owner who also runs a small retail operation selling grooming products. A coffee shop that does repairs on espresso machines as a side business. A gym that also runs a health food counter. A garage with a parts retail counter.

These operators currently have two options: force everything into one sector's logic (a repair shop trying to run café transactions through repair POS software is an ugly experience), or run two separate systems and pay for both.
