# Why the Hardware Model of POS Is Broken — and What Replaces It

**The POS industry has spent thirty years selling you a terminal. The terminal was never the point.**

---

## What You're Actually Buying When You Buy a POS Terminal

A traditional POS terminal is a computer. A slow, proprietary computer running specialised software, designed to do one thing — process transactions — and locked to a single vendor's ecosystem.

You pay for the hardware. You pay for the software subscription. You pay for the payment processing that runs through the vendor's merchant services. And when the hardware breaks, gets stolen, or becomes obsolete, you pay again.

In the UK, a full POS hardware bundle — terminal, card reader, receipt printer, barcode scanner, and stand — costs £800–2,000 from the main vendors. That's a capital expense, not a subscription. It hits the moment you open. And it starts depreciating immediately.

In Nigeria, Ghana, or Kenya, the same hardware often costs more in proportional terms: it's imported, potentially attracting import duties, shipped through unreliable logistics, and serviced by support teams that may be difficult to reach. When it breaks — and it breaks — replacement parts are harder to source and slower to arrive.

The hardware model of POS was designed for large retailers who could amortise the capital cost across high transaction volumes, who had IT teams to manage the equipment, and who could negotiate favourable terms with vendors. It was never designed for the small business owner who needs a reliable, affordable system to process transactions and understand their business.

## The Resilience Argument

Hardware fails. This is true everywhere, but it is more consequential in some markets than others.

In a UK high street environment, if your POS terminal breaks on a Friday afternoon, you call the vendor's support line, they dispatch a replacement, and it arrives by Monday. You lose one busy weekend. That's bad, but it's recoverable.

In Lagos, Nairobi, or Accra, the recovery timeline is different. Support may be available only through a local distributor. Parts may need to be sourced regionally. A replacement could take days or weeks. During that time, you are either processing transactions manually — with all the record-keeping problems that implies — or not processing them at all.

The software-on-your-own-device model eliminates this failure mode entirely.

If your phone breaks, is stolen, or stops working, you buy a replacement — a mid-range Android phone costs £80–120 and is available in any market town. You log into AskBiz on the new device. Your data is in the cloud: your transaction history, your product catalogue, your staff accounts, your business intelligence. Nothing is lost. You are back to full operation in the time it takes to set up a new phone.

For a one-person operation where the point-of-sale device is also the only device — which describes most informal sector traders in sub-Saharan Africa — this resilience is not a feature. It is the fundamental value proposition.

## The Receipt That Doesn't Need Paper

A traditional POS setup includes a receipt printer. The printer requires paper rolls, which are consumable, ongoing costs. It requires maintenance. It fails.

In markets where paper receipts are culturally expected — parts of formal retail in the UK and US — this matters. In markets where WhatsApp is the communication infrastructure — most of urban sub-Saharan Africa, much of Southeast Asia, significant parts of Latin America — paper receipts are already becoming obsolete.

AskBiz sends receipts via WhatsApp. The customer gets a receipt on the device they use for everything else — immediately, digitally, stored in their chat history. No paper. No printer. No paper roll costs.

For a market trader in Lagos or Nairobi who is currently not giving receipts at all because they don't have a printer, this is an upgrade in professional presentation at zero additional cost.

A busy café might want the POS running on two phones during peak hours — one at the counter and one for table-side ordering. With a hardware terminal, a second terminal costs a second hardware investment. With AskBiz, a second device just needs a seat assigned to it and an internet connection.

## The Right Model for the Right Market

The hardware model of POS made sense when smartphones didn't exist, when software couldn't run reliably in a browser, and when the cloud wasn't a viable storage and processing environment. Those conditions no longer apply.

The hardware model persists because the major POS vendors have hardware businesses — terminal manufacturers, payment hardware distributors — whose revenues depend on continued hardware sales. Moving to a software-only model would be commercially disrupting for them.

For the small business owner, that commercial consideration is irrelevant. What matters is cost, reliability, and the ability to run the system on the device they already have.

The hardware model is not broken because of any technical failure. It's broken because the world moved on and the POS industry hasn't.
