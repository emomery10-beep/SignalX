# The Missing Layer in African Fintech: Business Intelligence for the Informal Economy

**M-Pesa, Flutterwave, and Paystack solved how African SMEs receive money. Nobody solved what they should do with the information those payments contain.**

---

## The Payment Revolution That Stopped at the Receipt

The African fintech story is genuinely remarkable. M-Pesa, launched in Kenya in 2007, moved mobile money from novelty to infrastructure across East Africa. Flutterwave and Paystack have made accepting card and digital payments accessible to Nigerian merchants who previously had no path into the formal payment system. Chipper Cash, MoMo, Airtel Money — the continent now has payment rails that work.

But a payment confirmation is not business intelligence.

When a market trader in Nairobi receives an M-Pesa notification that reads "James Mwangi has sent you KSh 850," they know money arrived. They don't know which product drove that transaction. They don't know whether that customer has bought from them before. They don't know whether KSh 850 for that item is above or below market. They don't know how that transaction fits into the day's revenue pattern, the week's trend, or whether Tuesday afternoons are consistently their most profitable period.

The payment infrastructure captured the money. The intelligence layer — the layer that converts the fact of a payment into knowledge about the business — was never built.

---

## What Business Intelligence Actually Means

Business intelligence is not a luxury concept. It is, at its most basic, the ability to answer simple questions from data you already have:

- Which products make me the most money?
- Which staff member processes the most transactions?
- Is my revenue growing or declining, and at what rate?
- Which day of the week is my most profitable — not my busiest, but my most profitable?
- Are my prices in line with similar businesses?

Every formal business in a developed market answers these questions routinely, from systems that capture and analyse transaction data automatically. For the small business owner in Manchester or Minneapolis, this intelligence is embedded in the POS system they already use.

For the small business owner in Lagos or Kisumu, these questions have historically been unanswerable. Not because the transactions aren't happening — they absolutely are — but because there was no system capturing them in a way that could produce analysis.

The fintech revolution provided payment rails. It did not provide business intelligence. The gap between "I can accept digital payment" and "I understand my business" remains almost entirely open.

---

## Why the Gap Persists

The gap persists partly because it's commercially less obvious than payment infrastructure.

Payment is transactional — the value exchange is immediate and visible. A merchant accepts a payment; the money arrives; the service is clear. Investors understood this, funded it, and the market delivered excellent solutions.

Business intelligence is slower. The value accumulates over time as data builds up, patterns emerge, and decisions improve. The merchant who uses a business intelligence tool for six months is better at pricing, staffing, and stocking than the merchant who doesn't — but that compounding advantage is not immediately visible in the way that "I can now accept card payments" is.

This is why the intelligence layer was left behind. Not because it's less important — arguably it's more important, because good pricing decisions and operational efficiency determine whether a business is profitable, not just whether it can transact — but because its value is slower to manifest and harder to sell.

## The Data Quality Problem in Informal Markets

There's a deeper structural issue in data-scarce markets: formal data sources don't cover the informal sector.

Nielsen, Kantar, and the global market research firms do operate in African markets, but their coverage focuses on formal retail — supermarket chains, branded FMCG products, formally registered retailers. The informal sector — market traders, kiosk operators, mobile vendors, small service businesses — is either excluded entirely or captured through infrequent, expensive surveys with significant methodological limitations.

Trade associations that might publish pricing benchmarks often lack the coverage and update frequency to be operationally useful. Government statistics on the informal sector are estimates built on estimates.

The result is that a market trader in Accra trying to benchmark their prices against similar traders in their market has almost no formal data to draw on. They benchmark against their immediate neighbours by observation, and against their own sense of what the market will bear. It works, after a fashion — experienced traders develop good intuition — but intuition is not analysis, and it doesn't scale.

## The Layer on Top of Payments

Consider what the full intelligence stack looks like for a Lagos market trader who has adopted mobile payments and AskBiz:

**Layer 1 — Payment infrastructure (Flutterwave/Paystack/M-Pesa):** Accepts digital payments, money arrives in the account, payment confirmation issued.

**Layer 2 — Business intelligence (AskBiz):** Records what was sold, to whom, at what price, by which staff member, at which location. Compares to prior period. Flags anomalies. Benchmarks against similar traders. Generates daily brief. Tags transaction with GPS coordinates.

The payment layer tells you that money arrived. The intelligence layer tells you what drove it, whether it's normal, whether your pricing is right, and what to do tomorrow.

Neither layer replaces the other. A payment system without intelligence is a sophisticated receipt. An intelligence system without payment infrastructure has nothing to analyse.

The fintech ecosystem built the payment layer exceptionally well. The intelligence layer is what African SMEs need next.

## What This Means for SME Competitiveness

The practical consequence of having good business intelligence is that you make better decisions. Over time, better decisions compound into better business performance.

A trader who knows their highest-margin product category stocks it more heavily, displays it more prominently, and prices it more confidently than a trader who doesn't. A service business that knows which staff member produces the highest-quality work, as measured by repeat customer rates and review patterns, retains and rewards that person appropriately.

These advantages accumulate. A business that makes slightly better decisions every week, informed by data, will outperform a business making decisions by intuition — even if the intuition is excellent — over any meaningful time horizon.

In competitive urban markets in Lagos, Nairobi, Accra, and Johannesburg, where informal sector traders compete intensely for the same customer base, access to intelligence is becoming a meaningful differentiator. The traders who are first to understand their business at a data level will be better positioned than those who follow traditional practice.
