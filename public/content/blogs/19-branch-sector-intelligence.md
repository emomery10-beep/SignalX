**Knowing your total revenue is table stakes. Knowing which branch and which sector drove it, and why, is where decisions actually get made.**

## The Aggregation Problem

Most POS dashboards show you totals. Total revenue today. Total transactions this week. Total average ticket this month. These numbers are true, and they're useless for anything beyond a basic sense of whether business is up or down.

Here's why: totals obscure the variation that contains all the useful information.

If your business has two branches and total revenue is up 8% this month, is that good? It depends entirely on whether both branches are up, or one is up 25% and the other is down 12%. If it's the latter, you have a problem at Branch 2 that the total number is hiding.

If your business has two sectors — say, retail and repair — and combined revenue is flat, is the business performing well? It depends entirely on whether both sectors are flat, or one is growing strongly while the other is declining. If repair is up 20% and retail is down 20%, those two movements are cancelling each other out in the total — but they have completely different operational implications.

Aggregated data doesn't just fail to provide insight. It can actively mislead, by showing you a stable total that conceals divergent performance underneath.

## What Branch + Sector Filters Actually Enable

AskBiz applies Branch and Sector filter dropdowns consistently across every tab in the dashboard: Overview, Operations, Staff, Branches, Map, and Audit.

This consistency is more important than it might initially appear. Most business intelligence tools apply filters inconsistently — the overview dashboard might filter by branch, but the audit trail doesn't, so you can't trace a revenue anomaly at Branch 2 through to the specific transactions that caused it. The filter has to carry through the entire analytical journey.

When the same Branch + Sector selection applies across all tabs, you can follow a question all the way to its answer without losing context.

Example: The Overview tab shows that Branch 2's repair revenue is down 18% compared to last month. You switch to the Operations tab, still filtered to Branch 2, Repair sector — and you can see which specific job categories are down: screen replacements are holding, but battery replacements have dropped significantly. You switch to the Staff tab and see that one technician has processed far fewer jobs than usual in the last two weeks. You switch to the Audit trail, same filters, and you can see exactly which transactions that technician has processed, when, and whether there are anomalies in the record.

That analytical journey — from total to sector to job category to individual staff member to specific transactions — is only possible if the filters are consistent throughout. If the audit trail doesn't respect the Branch 2 / Repair filter, you've lost the thread.

## The Three-Branch, Two-Sector Scenario

Consider a business owner with three branches and operations across two sectors — retail and repair.

Without granular filtering, the questions they can answer are limited. Revenue is up or down. Maybe they can see each branch's total. That's roughly it.

With Branch + Sector filtering across all tabs, the questions they can answer in minutes include:

- How did Branch 2's repair revenue compare to Branch 1's last week?
- Which branch has the highest retail average ticket?
- Is the staffing cost at Branch 3 disproportionate to its revenue in either sector?
- Which branch had the most refunds last month in the repair sector?
- Which sector is driving the most growth across the business as a whole?
- Is there a specific staff member at Branch 1 whose repair job count is out of line with the others?
- On the map view, are Branch 2's geo-tagged transactions clustering in the expected area, or are they spreading further — indicating customers travelling from further away?

These are not esoteric questions. They are the basic operational intelligence that allows a multi-branch business to allocate resources effectively, identify problems before they compound, and make staffing and stocking decisions on evidence rather than intuition.

Without the filtering capability, answering most of these questions requires manual data extraction and calculation. With it, they're a matter of selecting the right filter combination and reading the answer.

## The Map View Dimension

The geo-tagged sales map in AskBiz adds a spatial dimension to branch and sector intelligence that standard tabular reporting can't provide.

Every transaction is tagged with the GPS coordinates of the device that processed it. On the map tab — filterable by branch and sector — those coordinates become pins, colour-coded by revenue value.

For a single-location business, this is a modestly useful tool. For a multi-location or multi-sector business, the map view becomes a genuine intelligence layer.

If Branch 1 and Branch 2 are both in the same city but different neighbourhoods, the map view shows the spatial distribution of transactions around each location. If Branch 2's transactions are clustering closer to the branch location than Branch 1's — i.e., customers are coming from a narrower catchment — that might indicate Branch 2 has a visibility or accessibility problem. Alternatively, if Branch 2's transactions are spread further than expected, that might indicate the location is drawing from a wider catchment and could support a higher marketing investment.

In the repair sector specifically, where some operators offer collection and delivery services, the map view can show where collection jobs are originating — identifying whether there are geographic patterns worth building around.

## The Staff Performance Intersection

The intersection of branch, sector, and staff performance is where multi-location businesses often find their most actionable insights.

Consider: you have four repair technicians across two branches. Looking at aggregate staff performance, they all look broadly similar — similar transaction counts, similar average job values. But filter by Branch 1 only, and two technicians appear: one is consistently 30% above the branch average on job value, the other is 15% below. Filter by Branch 2, and the pattern is reversed.

This kind of granular staff analysis is only possible when the filters carry through to the staff performance tab. Without that consistency, you're looking at blended data that conceals the variation.

The operational implications of finding this variation are significant. Is the high-performer at Branch 1 getting more complex jobs? Are they upselling repair add-ons more effectively? Are they faster, taking more jobs? Is the lower-performer at Branch 1 getting simpler jobs, or are they underperforming on comparable work? The data doesn't answer all of these questions — but it tells you which questions to ask, and where to look next.
