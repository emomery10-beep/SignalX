# Why UK Small Businesses Are Overpaying for Their POS System

**Most UK small business owners don't realise how much their POS system is actually costing them — until they do the maths.**

---

## The Bill You Never Fully Calculated

Let's start with Square, because it's the one most people reach for first. It looks affordable. The basic card reader is cheap or free. But once you're actually running a business through it, the costs layer up fast.

Square charges a transaction fee on every card payment — 1.75% on standard card-present transactions in the UK. On £30,000 of monthly revenue (a modest London café), that's £525 a month in transaction fees alone. Add a Square for Restaurants subscription at £60/month per location, and you're already at £585 before you've paid for staff, stock, or rent.

Lightspeed is worse. Their POS packages start around £89/month and climb steeply when you add loyalty, ecommerce, or analytics modules. Their hardware bundles — iPad, stand, card reader, receipt printer — run £800–£1,500 upfront.

For a repair shop, a salon, or a small retailer operating on tight margins in a high-street environment where rents are brutal, these fees are not trivial. They are, in many cases, the difference between profit and loss.

---

## What You're Actually Paying For

Here's what makes this frustrating: most of what those monthly fees buy is infrastructure you don't need.

Traditional POS vendors built their businesses around hardware. They sell you the terminal, the card reader, the receipt printer, sometimes the kitchen display screen. All of that has a cost to manufacture, ship, and support — and that cost gets baked into your monthly bill.

You're also paying for the reality that most POS software was designed in an era when a tablet was a luxury device. The assumption was that you'd buy their hardware bundle. The software was almost secondary.

That model made sense in 2012. It doesn't in 2025, when every business owner already carries a capable smartphone, most staff have tablets at home, and a decent Android device costs £150.

## Multi-Sector Without Multi-Systems

Here's something the established players have almost entirely missed: many small UK businesses don't fit neatly into one category.

A garage that runs a parts retail counter alongside its repair workshop. A café that sells branded merchandise and gift cards. A salon that also retails haircare products. A market stall operator who runs both food and non-food lines.

Until recently, covering these mixed operations meant either forcing everything into one sector's logic (and accepting the awkwardness) or running two separate POS systems (and accepting the cost and complexity).

## Making Tax Digital: The Compliance Angle

HMRC's Making Tax Digital programme is no longer optional. VAT-registered businesses must keep digital records and submit VAT returns through approved software. The MTD deadline for smaller businesses has already passed for most turnovers — and the net is widening.

Most affordable POS systems — Square included — don't natively export MTD VAT reports. You end up exporting a CSV, handing it to your accountant, and paying for the translation.

AskBiz includes an MTD VAT report button directly in the dashboard. It generates the figures HMRC needs, in the format they need, from the same data your POS is already capturing. No spreadsheets. No accountant required for basic VAT submission.

The audit trail — every transaction, every refund, every void, every staff action — is logged and searchable. If HMRC ever asks, you have it.

## Who This Actually Affects

The businesses most damaged by the current POS pricing model are exactly the ones with the least margin to absorb it: independent hospitality, high-street retail, and the repair/service economy.

A fish and chip shop on a Friday night processes hundreds of transactions. At 1.75% per transaction, those transaction fees compound fast. An independent bookshop running on thin margins can't absorb a £500 hardware investment every few years. A mobile phone repair shop with one or two staff members doesn't need enterprise software — it needs a reliable, cheap, intelligent system that runs on the device it already has.

These businesses are also, collectively, the backbone of UK high streets. The Treasury knows this. MTD compliance costs, high POS fees, and software complexity are all friction points that eat into viability.
